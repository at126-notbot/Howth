Sea Level Rise and Coastal Flooding in Howth

The increase in sea level on the coast of Ireland in the last 100 years is partly due to climate change. The global sea level has been rising at an accelerating rate over the past century, primarily due to the melting of land-based ice sheets and glaciers, and the thermal expansion of seawater as the oceans warm. The projected sea level rise on the coast of Ireland in the next 100 years will depend on several factors such as greenhouse gas emissions, ice melt, and ocean warming. The exact amount of sea level rise cannot be predicted with certainty, but various scientific studies have provided estimates based on different scenarios. The C-FLOOD provides projections of sea level rise for Ireland's coast up to 2100 based on a range of climate scenarios. According to their most recent report (2019), the projected sea level rise for the Irish coast ranges from 0.36 meters to 1.07 meters by 2100, depending on the emissions scenario. Extreme sea level events that are historically rare (once per century in the recent past) are projected to occur frequently (at least once per year) at many locations by 2050.
